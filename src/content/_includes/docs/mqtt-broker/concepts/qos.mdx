MQTT Quality of Service (QoS) defines the delivery contract between sender and receiver for each message.
It answers one question: what guarantee does the sender need that the receiver got the message?
Three levels are available, each a trade-off between reliability and the number of network round trips required.

The right choice matters because the wrong one has a cost in either direction. QoS 0 loses messages on unreliable
links. QoS 2 adds four-packet overhead to every message and can become a bottleneck at high throughput.
Most IoT deployments use QoS 1 as the default and reserve QoS 2 for the small subset of messages where duplicate
processing would cause real harm.

## Comparison

| | **QoS 0** | **QoS 1** | **QoS 2** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Guarantee** | At most once | At least once | Exactly once |
| **Delivery** | Best-effort | Guaranteed | Guaranteed |
| **Duplicates possible** | No | Yes | No |
| **Packets per message** | 1 (`PUBLISH`) | 2 (`PUBLISH`, `PUBACK`) | 4 (`PUBLISH`, `PUBREC`, `PUBREL`, `PUBCOMP`) |
| **Relative overhead** | Lowest | Medium | Highest |
| **Offline queuing for subscriber** | Never | Persistent session only | Persistent session only |

## QoS 0 — at most once

The message is sent once with no acknowledgement. If the network drops the packet, it is lost.

<ImageGallery images={[{ src: '/src/assets/schemas/qos-0.svg', alt: 'QoS 0 message flow: a single PUBLISH packet sent from sender to receiver with no acknowledgement.', caption: 'QoS 0 — at most once. The sender fires a single PUBLISH and forgets it.' }]} />

The sender removes the message from its queue immediately after sending. There is no retry and no confirmation.

**Use when** message loss is acceptable and recency matters more than completeness.
Typical examples: high-frequency sensor readings where the next value will arrive within seconds,
UI state broadcasts, or metrics where an occasional gap is tolerable.

## QoS 1 — at least once

The message is delivered at least once. The sender keeps it as in-flight until a matching `PUBACK` arrives.
Any unacknowledged message is resent at the next opportunity — at minimum when the session resumes with a
persistent client, as required by the MQTT specification.

<ImageGallery images={[{ src: '/src/assets/schemas/qos-1.svg', alt: 'QoS 1 message flow: PUBLISH from sender, then PUBACK from receiver. Sender retransmits until acknowledged.', caption: 'QoS 1 — at least once. The sender keeps the PUBLISH in-flight until it receives a PUBACK.' }]} />

If the `PUBACK` is lost in transit, the sender retransmits the `PUBLISH` with the `DUP` flag set. The receiver
may therefore process the same message more than once, so consumers must be **idempotent** — able to handle
duplicates safely.

**Use when** every message must arrive and your processing logic can tolerate or deduplicate repeated delivery.
Typical examples: telemetry from vehicles, security alerts, and device state changes.

## QoS 2 — exactly once

The message is delivered exactly once through a four-packet handshake that prevents both loss and duplication.

<ImageGallery images={[{ src: '/src/assets/schemas/qos-2.svg', alt: 'QoS 2 four-packet handshake: PUBLISH, PUBREC, PUBREL, PUBCOMP. Sender stores message, receiver stores packet ID, sender releases, receiver confirms release.', caption: 'QoS 2 — exactly once. A four-packet handshake guarantees neither loss nor duplication: PUBLISH → PUBREC → PUBREL → PUBCOMP.' }]} />

Neither side may reuse the packet ID until the full handshake completes. The receiver tracks the packet ID
from `PUBLISH` until it sends `PUBCOMP`, and any retransmitted `PUBLISH` arriving in that window is recognised
as a duplicate and discarded rather than re-processed. Both sides persist enough state to resume the handshake
after a crash, which is what closes the duplicate-delivery window across reconnects.

**Use when** duplicate processing causes real harm.
Typical examples: financial transactions, actuation commands on industrial machinery, and billing events.

<Aside type="note">
  QoS 2 guarantees exactly-once delivery **between each pair of hops**: publisher → broker, and broker → subscriber.
  End-to-end exactly-once across both hops requires both sides to use QoS 2.
</Aside>

## QoS downgrade

When the publisher's QoS and the subscriber's QoS differ, TBMQ delivers the message at the **lower of the two levels**.

<ImageGallery images={[{ src: '/src/assets/schemas/qos-downgrade.svg', alt: 'QoS downgrade: a publisher sending PUBLISH at QoS 1 and a subscriber subscribed at QoS 2 receive the message at QoS 1 — the lower of the two levels.', caption: 'When publisher and subscriber QoS levels differ, the broker delivers at the lower of the two — here a QoS 2 subscription only gets QoS 1 because the publisher used QoS 1.' }]} />

A QoS 2 subscription therefore does not guarantee exactly-once delivery when the publisher sends at QoS 0 or
QoS 1. Design for the actual delivered level, not the subscribed level.

Note that TBMQ grants the QoS the subscriber requested in `SUBACK` — the downgrade is applied per message at
delivery time, not when the subscription is created.

## QoS and session persistence

Whether undelivered QoS 1 and QoS 2 messages survive a disconnect depends on the subscriber's session type.
With a non-persistent (clean) session, the broker keeps no offline state at all, so any messages that arrive
while the client is offline are dropped regardless of QoS. With a persistent session, the broker queues QoS 1
and QoS 2 messages and delivers them when the client reconnects.

| Subscriber session | QoS 0 | QoS 1 / QoS 2 |
|---|---|---|
| Non-persistent | Dropped while offline | Dropped while offline |
| Persistent | Dropped while offline | Queued, delivered on reconnect |

QoS 0 messages are never queued for offline subscribers, even in a persistent session: the broker delivers them
live or drops them. Likewise, a message published at QoS 0 is never persisted on the broker — even if some
subscribers use a persistent session — because the publisher has no expectation of guaranteed delivery.

## Choosing a level

| Situation | Recommended level |
|---|---|
| High-frequency sensor data; loss of one reading is harmless | QoS 0 |
| Device telemetry that must reach the backend | QoS 1 |
| Commands sent to a device (on/off, setpoint) | QoS 1 with idempotent handling |
| Financial or billing events | QoS 2 |
| Industrial actuation where duplicate execution causes damage | QoS 2 |
| Analytics consumer that must process every event exactly once | QoS 2 on both publisher and subscriber |
